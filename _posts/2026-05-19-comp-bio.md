As I’ll be moving away from computational biology for at least some time, I wanted to reflect a bit about my journey in the field and where I hope it moves next. For context, I joined it in 2018 for an internship with Andrei Zinovyev at Institut Curie, and did my PhD under his supervision between 2020 and 2023. At that time, we were excited about things like adapting auto-encoder architectures to RNA-seq data for dropout denoising[^dca], integrating single-cell expression across patients[^harmony], or predicting RNA velocity from unspliced transcripts[^velocity]. Most of the methods back then were based on classical modeling, borrowing deep learning pieces here and there, and generally grounded in hypotheses specifically tailored to the data.

Fast forward to 2026, where many canonical ML fields have had their "GPT moment” and were able to successfully tame the transformer architecture to unlock genuine transformative applications and data/compute scaling laws; despite the efforts of top labs and brilliant researchers, computational biology (at least, transcriptomics) is not yet one of them. The literature has been flooded with attempts over the last few years to generalize the LLM pretraining recipe (“throw every data available into a large transformer and let it cook”) to transcriptomics data, and we have yet to observe striking benefits of this approach over gene selection + PCA and other canonical methods.

![The streetlight effect]({{ '/assets/images/streetlight-effect.jpg' | relative_url }})

Before they fade away, I have gathered a handful of ideas and questions I still have in mind that may (or may not) be part of the recipe for the next generation of models. Maybe some of them will resonate!

## We urgently need better model specifications

Now that the dust has settled a little bit, it might be worth taking time to really think about what the real-life use cases of these models are. How will they be leveraged for practitioners and businesses? Embed samples to train classifiers? Extract trajectories? Discover biomarkers? Identify rare cell types? And is it even possible to get them all in one model? Ask 5 model makers, get 7 different answers.

Scienta’s internal benchmark (see our ICLR26-LMRL publication[^scienta-bench]) was for us a first necessary step to characterise our needs, in order for our EVA model to provide real added value. I hope to see the field develop a solid benchmark culture in the coming years, with, if possible, open, measurable, and reproducible evaluation, instead of the same CELLxGENE[^cellxgene] cell type prediction, or transcriptomics perturbation featuring the same aberrant, 67-chromosome cell line. Without systematic evaluation, we cannot measure improvement or compare approaches; as we say in France, “*autant pisser dans un violon*” (pointless effort).

## Model pretraining objectives deserve more attention

I feel like many of us have rushed headlong into the LLM pretraining recipe, adding a few convenient hypotheses here and there to make it “work” (no blame, it was the reasonable first thing to try). But if you think a bit, BERT[^bert] learns text embeddings from non-vectorized data, and is used as a text encoder. GPT[^gpt] learns to predict the next token from a prefix and is the core of conversational agents. DINO[^dino] learns well-behaved embedding spaces for image data, and is used as an image encoder.

Transcriptomics foundation models? They learn coexpression patterns between genes, and we hope that it will yield good sample embeddings. To me, that alone is a bit wishful thinking: how does modeling gene-gene coexpression incentivize the model to structure a well-behaved, higher-level latent space, encoding things such as tissue state, clinical covariates, cell processes, etc? To me, the transfer is not obvious; it raises the natural question: then, how do we define an SSL pretraining objective that forces the model to construct higher-level representations that are relevant to downstream applications? I don’t know the answer, of course, but defining the right pretraining objective is in my opinion the strongest lever of all.

## Gene-as-a-token may not be a free hypothesis

Several approaches, such as CellTok[^celltok] and Arc’s STACK[^stack], have been exploring representations of cells that would be more suited for transformers than the “gene as a token” paradigm, replacing that with “cell tokens” or “gene modules”. I believe that this approach is the way forward for transcriptomics foundation models, as it just seems so natural to do, similar to BPE in NLP or VQ-VAE in vision. Add to that the prohibitive cost of performing full self-attention across the whole 20,000+ genes transcriptome, and you get why approaches like CellTok and STACK really land a point.

But how? Transcriptomics looks both continuous and not really structured, unlike text and image, so NLP and vision approaches do not cleanly transfer. You cannot easily rely on local structure like in BPE ("merge most frequent bigram") or in VQ-VAE ("map each patch embedding to the closest codebook word"). Without spoiling the whole thing, I can say that the team at Scienta has been working hard on this topic lately, and there may be some things in the pipeline for later this year!

## Why should we use LLMs as a blueprint in the first place?

Most of the methods are based on the LLM paradigm, but transcriptomics may arguably be at least as close to vision as it is to language. Many things don't translate cleanly from NLP to transcriptomics, while in vision, you get some vectorized, noisy data as an input, and you want to learn more useful, stable embeddings of these data that you can leverage with downstream models for higher-level tasks.

| Feature                  | NLP             | Vision               | Transcriptomics    |
| ------------------------ | --------------- | -------------------- | ------------------ |
| Native input form        | Discrete tokens | Continuous           | Continuous         |
| Vectorized out of the box| No              | Yes                  | Yes                |
| Magnitudes meaningful?   | No              | Yes                  | Yes                |
| Measurement noise        | None            | Moderate             | High               |
| Needs normalization?     | No              | Yes                  | Yes                |
| Upstream tokenization    | Required (BPE)  | Optional (VQ-VAE/GAN)| Open question      |

So, if SSL approaches like contrastive learning, Barlow Twins[^barlow], DINO, etc., are a little bit painful to train, it looks like they proved useful tackling data and problems very analogous to the ones we’re facing with transcriptomics. There have been some attempts here and there, but nowhere near the traction pulled by LLM-like approaches, and I'm expecting to see this revisited in the coming years!

## Data is not the primary issue

Unlike others in the field, I’m not too gloomy with respect to available data. With reasonable effort, we can gather bulk and microarray samples in the millions (ARCHS4[^archs4]), and single-cells in the hundreds of millions (CELLxGENE[^cellxgene], scBaseCount[^scbasecount]). Yes, it is noisy, but it still is a lot and already covers plenty of biological conditions (sure, more will come with time and corpora will grow, no doubt about it).

I’m genuinely convinced that the single biggest lever today is not “more data”; many model makers even report that they do not even conduct a full pass on their pretraining corpus, which to me shows that we are far today from making the best out of what's available due to a lackluster modeling pipeline (data representation, model architecture and/or pretraining objective). I would say that if we aren’t able to train any satisfying model with the current data regimes, then the picture looks pretty grim. So, my impression is that we need to crack the modeling and transfer part first, which can then be scaled with more compute and more data.

## Sutton-pilled or prior-pilled, choose your poison

Inductive bias approaches are becoming more and more trendy, to circumvent our model and data issues: knowledge graph-augmented transformers, GO-based neural networks, post-trained LLM approaches... Historically, the "Bitter Lesson of AI"[^bitter] has warned us about trying to improve our machine learning models by hand-crafting their priors, and to instead let them learn their representations by scaling compute and data on a properly defined learning task. *But Biology is special...* Or is it?

Option (a), we do not have enough data to scale yet, so we have to rely on injecting temporary subpar priors into our models to compensate. Option (b), we are not ready to accept a modeling of Biology that does not fit our priors. I'll leave it up to the reader to define what's most likely.

This connects to the first sections: what are we building these models for? Do we want models that are optimized to fit the 1% we know about Biology, or should we consider observed data as the ground truth plus observation noise, and accept that model representations can diverge from our current understanding? And if some priors can indeed survive scale, then where should they be encoded? In the architecture? The loss? The data curation and mixture? The post-training? All these questions show the cultural friction between machine learning and biomedical applications, and I feel like we’re far from having a final answer.

## And what about multimodality?

This is the one that still puzzles me. Biological data is multimodal, yes, everybody agrees on that, and that considering only one modality at a time is missing out on a major part of the signal. But does it mean that there is no hope of training capable unimodal encoders, because the information is too partial, and we should immediately focus our efforts on multimodality? Some actors in the field defend this thesis. Or is having good unimodal encoders first a necessary stepping stone before moving on to multimodal models? Currently, that's what most model makers are doing.

I could see a case for both; CLIP[^clip] showed us that aligning spaces from two good encoders is a workable recipe, but it’s also probably easier to define the concept of “same thing” in NLP and vision. Nonetheless, it's worth adding that generating paired multimodal data in Biology is much more complex and expensive than scraping co-occurring image-text pairs à la CLIP. This to me is a pragmatic reason why it's worth pushing a bit more to make unimodal work first: it's a good way to derisk the whole paradigm without committing to a major cost upfront.
