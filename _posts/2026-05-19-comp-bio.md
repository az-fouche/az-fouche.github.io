As I’ll be moving away from computational biology for at least some time, I wanted to reflect a bit about my journey in the field and where I hope it moves next. For context, I joined it in 2018 for an internship with Andrei Zinovyev at Institut Curie, and did my PhD under his supervision between 2020 and 2023. At that time, we were excited about things like adapting auto-encoder architectures to RNA-seq data for dropout denoising[^dca], integrating single-cell expression across patients[^integration], or predicting RNA velocity from unspliced transcripts[^velocity]. Most of the methods back then were based on classical modeling, borrowing deep learning pieces here and there, and generally grounded in hypotheses specifically tailored to the data.

Fast forward to 2026, where many canonical ML fields have had their "GPT moment” and were able to successfully tame the transformer architecture to unlock genuine transformative applications and data/compute scaling laws; despite the efforts of top labs and brilliant researchers, computational biology (at least, transcriptomics) is not yet one of them. The literature has been flooded with attempts over the last few years to generalize the LLM pretraining recipe (“throw every data available into a large transformer and let it cook”) to transcriptomics data, and we have yet to observe striking benefits of this approach over gene selection + PCA and other canonical methods.

![The streetlight effect]({{ '/assets/images/streetlight-effect.jpg' | relative_url }})
*LLM light is bright, but the key may not be there.*

Before they fade away, I have gathered a handful of opinions, ideas and questions I still have in mind that may (or may not) be relevant for the recipe of the next generation of models. Maybe some of them will resonate!

## We urgently need to standardize model evaluation

Now that the dust has settled a little bit, it might be worth taking time to really think about what the real-life use cases of these models are. How will they be leveraged for practitioners and businesses? Embed samples to train classifiers? Extract trajectories? Discover biomarkers? Identify rare cell types? And is it even possible to get them all in one model? Ask 5 model makers, get 7 different answers. Everyone in the field is super excited about trying a new pretraining objective, a new dataset, or a different model architecture, yet no standard set of evaluation tasks exists to measure progress.

I believe that currently, the most valuable contributions you can bring to the field are standardized open benchmarks that specify the various problems we are trying to crack with these models; vision had ImageNet, NLP had GLUE, structural biology had CASP. There have been initiatives here and there (scIB[^scib], virtual cell challenge...), but they stay at the fundamental level and nothing has really stuck yet. Of course, working on such tasks is not super sexy in comparison to developing a new model, but this would go a very long way in what the field will be able to achieve in the next few years.

At Scienta, we built the I&I benchmark[^scienta-bench] to pin down concrete needs and define tasks with real added value; it should be released publicly later this year. I hope to see others grow the field's benchmark culture in the coming years. We won't get the models very far if we stick to cell type prediction on CELLxGENE[^cellxgene], transcriptomics perturbation featuring the same aberrant, 67-chromosome K562 cell line, or cherry-picked tasks from unreleased internal evaluations. Without systematic benchmarking, we do not have a clear direction, and cannot measure any improvement or compare approaches; as we say in France, “*autant pisser dans un violon*” (pointless effort).

## Model pretraining objectives deserve more attention

I feel like many of us have rushed headlong into the LLM pretraining recipe, adding a few convenient hypotheses here and there to make it “work” (no blame, it was the reasonable first thing to try). But if you think a bit, BERT[^bert] learns text embeddings from non-vectorized data, and is used as a text encoder. GPT[^gpt] learns next token prediction and is the core of conversational agents. DINO[^dino] learns well-behaved embedding spaces for image data, and is used as an image encoder.

Transcriptomics foundation models? They learn coexpression patterns between genes, and we hope that it will incentivize the model to "learn the latent Biology". But, what's the rationale? how does modeling gene-gene coexpression motivate the model to structure a well-behaved, higher-level latent space, encoding things such as tissue state, clinical covariates, cell processes, etc? This comes back to the tasks we're wanting to tackle with these models. If we want to discover new gene-gene interaction patterns, maybe the pretraining objective can make sense -- though it's not super clear to me what's the added value compared to a coexpression matrix. For higher-level tasks such as learning a sample/patient embedding space, the transfer is way less obvious; why would the model come up with anything else beyond an encoding of the gene-gene coexpression patterns?

This then raises the natural question: then, how do we define SSL pretraining objectives that force the model to construct higher-level representations that are relevant to downstream applications? Can we chase a "Foundation model of Biology", with a task holistic enough to capture many intertwined information layers such as NTP in NLP? Or will the field need to rely on different models for different use cases, because you can't simultaneously promote good pathway and patient representations? I don’t know the answer, of course, but as you'll see in the next sections I'd put a coin on moving away from the gene-as-a-token LLM inspiration, and instead explore vision-derived approaches.

## Gene-as-a-token may not be a free lunch

Several approaches, such as CellTok[^celltok] and Arc’s STACK[^stack], have been exploring representations of cells that would be more suited for transformers than the “gene as a token” paradigm, replacing that with “cell tokens” or “gene modules”. Approaches like *Hidden in Plain Sight*[^hidden] suggest that instead of masking individual genes, we should instead mask entire gene groups based on covariance patterns in order to build higher-level relationships between gene expression modules, in the same way that you want to mask a contiguous patch in an image rather than random pixels here and there.

I believe that this is the way forward for transcriptomics foundation models, similar to BPE in NLP or VQ-VAE in vision. Add to that the prohibitive cost of performing full self-attention across the whole 20,000+ gene transcriptome, and you get why these recent approaches in transcriptomics make a strong case. And ideally, you'd want an unsupervised, automatic algorithm similar to BPE in NLP.

But how? Transcriptomics is both continuous and unstructured, unlike text and image, so NLP and vision approaches do not cleanly transfer. You cannot easily rely on local structure like in BPE ("merge most frequent bigram") or in VQ-VAE ("map each patch embedding to the closest codebook word"). Without spoiling the whole thing, I can say that the team at Scienta has been working hard on this topic lately, and there are some things in the pipeline for later this year (so I'll leave it as a teaser)!

## Why should we use LLMs as a blueprint in the first place?

Probably because of the hype generated by the huge successes in NLP, most of the transcriptomics foundation models are based on the LLM paradigm, yet transcriptomics is arguably closer to vision than it is to natural language. Many things don't translate cleanly from NLP to transcriptomics: one is a structured sequence of discrete tokens, the other is a noisy bag of redundant, unstructured transcripts. In vision, you get some vectorized data as an input with some level of noise, and you want models to learn useful, stable embeddings of these data that you can leverage with downstream models for higher-level tasks. That to me looks like exactly the specifications of a transcriptomics foundation model!

| Feature                  | NLP             | Vision               | Transcriptomics    |
| ------------------------ | --------------- | -------------------- | ------------------ |
| Native input form        | Discrete tokens | Continuous           | Continuous         |
| Vectorized out of the box| No              | Yes                  | Yes                |
| Measurement noise        | None            | Moderate             | High               |
| Needs normalization?     | No              | Yes                  | Yes                |
| Upstream tokenization    | Required (BPE)  | Optional (VQ-VAE)    | Open question      |

So, if SSL approaches like contrastive learning, Barlow Twins[^barlow], DINO, etc., are known to be harder to train than their LLM counterparts due to instability and hyperparameter sensitivity, it looks like they proved useful tackling data and problems very analogous to the ones we’re facing with transcriptomics. There have been some attempts here and there, but nowhere near the traction pulled by LLM-like approaches, and I'm expecting to see this revisited in the coming years!

The main difficulty I think is to clearly define what's an *augmentation*; in vision you can crop, reshade, darken, blur, or rotate your image, while in transcriptomics the valid transformations are less obvious and the data is more noisy. I have seen BYOL and Barlow-Twins adapted to single-cell data with encouraging results[^ssl-theis], using distributional resampling and dropout as augmentations. This to me is a promising lead to follow.

## Data is not the primary issue

Unlike others in the field, I’m not too gloomy about what we can do with the data available if we can come up with better objectives. Thanks to massive open-source initiatives, model makers can access bulk and microarray samples in the millions (ARCHS4[^archs4]), and single cells in the hundreds of millions (CELLxGENE[^cellxgene], scBaseCount[^scbasecount]). Sure, more data will come gradually with time and corpora will grow, transcriptomics is noisy and redundant, and perturbation data would be a great addition to teach models causality. Still, I think we already have a lot of data to build a first generation of useful foundation models, that can be exposed to plenty of biological conditions, tissues, cell types, species, diseases, technologies, etc.

Most notably, I have noticed that many model makers report that they do not even conduct a full pass on their pretraining corpus. This to me reinforces my stance that we are far from making the best out of what's available, mainly due to the limitations in the modeling part (data representation and pretraining objective). We may be trying to disguise giant coexpression matrices into GPT-like general learners.

So, if I’m genuinely convinced that the single biggest lever today is not “more data”, I would go as far as saying that if we aren’t able to train any satisfying model at current data scales, then the picture looks pretty grim for the future of foundation models for Biology. My impression is that we absolutely need to crack the modeling and transfer learning part of the problem first, before we can scale with more compute and more data.

## Sutton-pilled or prior-pilled, choose your poison

Inductive bias approaches are increasingly trendy, to circumvent our model and data issues: knowledge graph-augmented transformers, GO-based neural networks, biology-specialized LLMs... Historically though, the "Bitter Lesson of AI"[^bitter] has warned us about trying to improve our machine learning models by hand-crafting their priors, and to instead let them learn their representations by scaling compute and data on a properly defined learning task. *But Biology is special...* Or is it?

Option (a), we do not have enough data to scale yet, so we have to rely on priors as a crutch. Option (b), we strongly value models that validate our priors about Biology so we try to engrave these priors in our pipelines. Option (c), these priors are necessary because the data alone does not contain the information. This to me reveals a very interesting tension in the domain, and a striking difference with SSL approaches elsewhere. In NLP or in vision, models have been trained to fit the structure of the data, with unapologetically simple pretraining objectives: data is the ground truth. In computational Biology, we do not seem to trust the data the same way, to the point we feel the need to incorporate various priors in our modeling, with no guarantee they helps at all.

This connects to the first sections: what are we building these models for? Do we want models that are optimized to fit the what we know about Biology, or should we always consider data as the ground truth, and accept that model representations can be more abstract and diverge from our current understanding? And if some priors can indeed survive scale, then where should they be encoded? In the architecture? The loss? The data curation and mixture? The post-training? All these questions show the cultural friction between machine learning and biomedical applications, and I feel like we’re far from having a final answer. Personally, I'd argue for an Occam's razor approach during pretraining (don't bake any additional priors into a general learner unless you can be fairly certain that it helps), and explore options to augment the model with priors in post-training.

## And what about multimodality?

This is the one that still puzzles me. Biological data is multimodal -- everybody agrees on that, and that considering only one modality at a time leaves a lot of signal on the table. But does it mean that there is no hope of training capable unimodal encoders, because the information is too partial, and we should immediately focus our efforts on multimodality? A part of the field defends this thesis. Or is having good unimodal encoders first a necessary stepping stone before moving on to multimodal models? Currently, that's what most model makers are doing.

I could see a case for both; CLIP[^clip] showed us that aligning spaces from two good encoders is a workable recipe, but it’s also probably easier to define the concept of “same thing” in NLP and vision. A photo of a cat and the caption "cat" clearly refer to the same thing, while defining the concept of "same thing" between a histology patch and a transcriptomics profile is subject to more questions. Also, generating paired multimodal data in Biology is much more complex and expensive than scraping co-occurring image-text pairs à la CLIP. To me, this is a pragmatic reason why it's worth pushing a bit more to make unimodal work first: it's a good way to derisk the whole paradigm and nail the pretraining recipes, without committing to a major cost upfront.
