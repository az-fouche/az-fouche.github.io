As I’ll be moving away from computational biology for at least some time, I wanted to reflect a bit about my journey in the field, and where I hope it moves next. For context, I joined in 2018 during an internship with Andrei Zinovyev at Institut Curie, and did my PhD under his supervision between 2020 and 2023. At that time, we were excited about things like adapting auto-encoder architectures to RNA-seq data for dropout denoising[^dca], integrating single-cell expression across patients[^integration], or predicting RNA velocity from unspliced transcripts[^velocity]. Most of the methods back then were based on classical modeling, sometimes borrowing deep learning pieces here and there, and were generally grounded in hypotheses specifically tailored to the data and the problem.

Fast forward to 2026. Many canonical ML fields have had their "GPT moment” and were able to successfully tame the transformer architecture to unlock genuine transformative applications by scaling data and compute. Despite the efforts of top labs and brilliant researchers, computational biology is not yet one of them. The literature has been flooded with attempts over the last few years to generalize the LLM pretraining recipe  to transcriptomics data (=throw every data available into a large transformer with a SSL objective, and let it cook), and we have yet to observe striking benefits of this approach over canonical methods such as gene selection + PCA.

![The streetlight effect]({{ '/assets/images/streetlight-effect.jpg' | relative_url }})
*LLM light is bright, but the key may not be there.*

So before they fade away, I have gathered a handful of opinions, ideas and questions I still have in mind which may (or may not) be relevant to build the next generation of models. Maybe some of them will resonate!

## We urgently need to standardize model evaluation

Now that the dust has settled a little bit, it might be worth taking some time to really think about the real-life use cases of these models. How will they be leveraged by practitioners and businesses to bring value? Embed samples to train classifiers? Extract patient trajectories? Discover new biomarkers? Identify new cell types? And is it even possible to get all of these into one model? Ask 5 model makers, get 7 different answers; everyone in the field is super excited about tring a new dataset or a different model architecture, yet no standard evaluation exists to measure progress.

I believe that currently, the most valuable contribution you can bring to the field is a standardized open benchmark that specifies one of the problems we are trying to crack with these models; vision had ImageNet, NLP had GLUE, structural biology had CASP... What do we have? There have been notable attempts here and there like scIB[^scib], or the virtual cell challenge, but they stayed at the *capability* level rather than probing real added value, and none really stuck yet as a standard evaluation. I do not pretend this is easy; defining benchmark tasks requires to properly specify what we want to optimize these models for, which as aforementioned is a genuinely difficult task. Also, for many tasks in computational Biology it is hard to define what is "ground truth" without jumping into costly experiments, and what is the right level to probe the model transfer capability (gene? sample? patient?).

But, this reflexion around the utilization of these models is an unskippable step if we want the field to evolve from the "proof of concept" stage to genuinely useful models that provide value and unlock use cases. I understand that working on defining rigorous, systematic benchmarks is not as exciting as developing new models, but this would go a long way in terms of what the field can achieve in the next few years. At Scienta, we built an I&I benchmark[^scienta-bench] to pin down concrete needs and define tasks with real added value, like clinical response prediction or disease stratification; it should be released publicly later this year.

I hope to see the field grow a benchmark culture in the coming years. I am convinced that we won't get very useful models if we stick to tasks like cell type prediction on CELLxGENE[^cellxgene], transcriptomics perturbation featuring the same aberrant, 67-chromosome K562 cell line, or cherry-picked claims measured in Pearson over unreleased internal evaluations. Without systematic benchmarking, we do not have a clear direction, and we cannot measure any failure, or improvement, or even compare approaches; as we say in France, “*autant pisser dans un violon*” (pointless effort).

## Self-supervised learning, but to learn what?

Many of us have rushed headlong into the LLM pretraining recipe, rounding corners and adding a few convenient hypotheses here and there to fit the transcriptomics data in (no blame, it was 100% the first reasonable thing to try). But the results aren't great, and if you think of it: BERT[^bert] learns text embeddings from non-vectorized data, and is used as a text encoder. GPT[^gpt] learns next token prediction and is the core of conversational agents. DINO[^dino] learns well-behaved embedding spaces for image data, and is used as an image encoder. In addition to having pretraining tasks that have more immediate transfer to downstream use cases, all these tasks have a richness gradient in them, in the sense that solving them requires the model to develop higher-level world representations (while a task at the character-level or pixel-level would be closer to local denoising).

Transcriptomics foundation models? They learn coexpression patterns between genes, and we hope that it will incentivize the model to "learn the latent Biology". But, what's the rationale here? How does modeling gene-gene coexpression motivate the model to structure a well-behaved, higher-level latent space, encoding things such as tissue state, clinical covariates, cell processes, etc? This comes back to the tasks we're wanting to tackle with these models. If we want to discover new gene-gene interaction patterns, maybe such coexpression pretraining objective can make sense. For higher-level tasks such as learning a rich sample/patient embedding space that would behave better than the gene expression space, the transfer becomes even less obvious; why would the model come up with anything richer than encoding the gene-gene coexpression patterns? We are hoping giant coexpression matrices behave like GPT-like general learners.

This raises the natural question: then, how do we define SSL pretraining objectives that force the model to construct higher-level representations that are relevant to downstream applications? And is there any task optimizable in SSL over transcriptomics data that approaches the richness of what we saw in NLP or vision? Can we even chase a "Foundation model of Biology", with a task holistic enough that it captures many intertwined information layers, such as NTP in NLP? Or will the field need to build different models for different use cases, because you can't simultaneously promote good pathway and patient representations?

There is a last possibility, that transcriptomics data alone is not suited to train models in SSL, and needs to be either replaced or augmented by other data types (perturbation, spatial, proteomics, chromatin assays...); basically, a need for SSL objectives to be multimodal. Some in the field are pushing for that, but it raises a lot of other issues (around paired data scarcity, multimodal alignment, SSL across modalities, missing modalities, etc) and nothing convincing has yet come out of this branch either. So, as you can see, I don’t know the answer for SSL in transcriptomics. As you'll see in the next sections, I'd put a coin on moving away from the gene-as-a-token LLM inspiration similar to how NLP moved away from character-level representations, and also suggest to consider vision-derived approaches that may be closer to the encoder paradigm we're chasing.

## The gene-as-a-token hypothesis is not a free lunch

Several approaches recently, such as CellTok[^celltok] and Arc’s STACK[^stack], have been exploring representations of cells that would be more suited for transformers than the “gene as a token” paradigm, replacing that with “cell tokens” or “gene modules”. On the objective side, *Hidden in Plain Sight*[^hidden] suggests that instead of masking individual genes, we should instead mask entire gene groups based on covariance patterns in order to build higher-level relationships between gene expression modules, in the same way that you want to mask a contiguous patch in an image rather than random pixels here and there.

Defining a better-behaved, coarser-grained representations of the transcriptome represents to me the way forward for transcriptomics foundation models, similar to what BPE did in NLP, or VQ-VAE in vision. Add to that the prohibitive cost of performing full self-attention across the whole 20,000+ gene transcriptome, and you get why these recent approaches in transcriptomics make a strong case. And ideally, you'd want an unsupervised, automatic algorithm similar to BPE in NLP.

But how? Transcriptomics is both continuous and unstructured, unlike text and image, so NLP and vision approaches do not cleanly transfer. You cannot easily rely on local structure like in BPE ("merge most frequent bigram") or in VQ-VAE ("map each patch embedding to the closest codebook word"). Without spoiling the whole thing, all I can say is that the team at Scienta has been working hard on this topic lately, and there are some things in the pipeline for later this year (so I'll leave it as a teaser)!

## LLMs may not be the right blueprint

Probably because of the hype generated by the huge successes in NLP, most of the transcriptomics foundation models are based on the LLM paradigm, yet models we need for transcriptomics may arguably be closer to vision models rather than to LLMs. The translation from NLP to transcriptomics has annoying quirks: LLMs work on a structured sequence of discrete tokens, and learn to fill the gaps based on the tokens order and the local and global context. This is quite difficult to adapt to transcriptomics. In vision, your input data already comes vectorized, and models are optimized to learn robust embeddings using augmentation-based SSL, which can be leveraged in higher-level tasks. That to me looks much more alike to the specifications of a transcriptomics foundation model!

| Feature                  | NLP             | Vision               | Transcriptomics    |
| ------------------------ | --------------- | -------------------- | ------------------ |
| Native input form        | Discrete tokens | Continuous           | Pseudo-continuous  |
| Vectorized out of the box| No              | Yes                  | Yes                |
| Measurement noise        | None            | Moderate             | High               |
| Needs normalization?     | No              | Yes                  | Yes                |
| Upstream tokenization    | Required (BPE)  | Optional (VQ-VAE)    | Open question      |
| Structure                | Sequential      | 2D spatial           | Set-like           |
| Sparsity                 | Dense           | Dense                | Sparse             |

So, if SSL approaches like contrastive learning, Barlow Twins[^barlow], DINO, etc., are known to be harder to train than their LLM counterparts due to training instability and hyperparameter sensitivity, it looks like they proved useful tackling data and problems very analogous to the ones we’re facing with transcriptomics. There have been some attempts here and there, but nowhere near the traction pulled by LLM-like approaches, though I'm expecting to see this statement revisited in the coming years. Combining that with set transformer architectures could also be a reasonable call: rather than trying to force a sense of order and sequential structure in fundamentally unordered, non-sequential data, embracing its specificities and adapting the model architecture to it seems a good approach -- though, it requires rethinking a large part of the current paradigm.

The main difficulty I think for these approaches is to clearly define what's an *augmentation*; in vision you can crop, reshade, darken, blur, or rotate your image, while in transcriptomics the invariants are less abundant and less obvious, and the data is less interpretable and more noisy. I have seen BYOL and Barlow-Twins adapted to single-cell data with early but encouraging results[^ssl-theis], using distributional resampling and dropout as augmentations. This, to me, is a promising lead to follow.

## I do not believe that data is the current bottleneck

Unlike others in the field, I’m not too gloomy about what could do with the data available, if we can ever come up with better pretraining objectives. Thanks to massive open-source initiatives, model makers can access bulk and microarray samples in the millions (ARCHS4[^archs4]), and single cells in the hundreds of millions (CELLxGENE[^cellxgene], scBaseCount[^scbasecount]). Sure, more data will come gradually with time and corpora will grow, sure transcriptomics is noisy and redundant, sure perturbation data would be a great addition to teach models causality. Still, I think we already have a plentiful of data to build a first generation of useful foundation models, that have been exposed to many biological conditions, tissues, cell types, species, diseases, technologies, etc.

Most notably, I have noticed that some model makers report that they do not conduct a full pass on their pretraining corpus. Also, while some teams have reported scaling behavior with dataset size, model size, or compute here and there, the validity of these claims is not settled in the community to this day. If more data or more compute were the bottleneck, I'd expect teams to routinely report clear compute-optimal scaling laws, and a connection between pretraining validation loss and downstream tasks, which is not the case. This reinforces my stance that we are far from making the best out of what's available, mainly due to the limitations in the modeling part (data representation and pretraining objective). Either the data is somewhat redundant (mostly true for single-cell data, much less for bulk/microarray), or our pretraining tasks are too simple and do not incentivize models to learn the deep data structure. In both cases, the single biggest lever today does not look to me that “we need more data”. Since the first generation of models, pretraining data has gone up by almost an order of magnitude, yet we did not observe striking improvements in performance -- but this should be taken with a grain of salt given the lack of systematic benchmarking.

So, I would go as far as saying that if we aren’t able to train any satisfying model (i.e., better than PCA) with millions of samples and hundreds of millions of single-cells, then the picture looks pretty grim for the future of transcriptomics foundation models. My impression is rather that we haven't cracked the modeling part yet; more data volume and variety won't help while we haven't figured out more precisely the recipe of how to build a general learner.

## Sutton-pilled or bio-pilled, choose your poison

Adding biology-based inductive bias to the sauce is becoming increasingly trendy, as a bandaid to circumvent our modeling and data issues: knowledge graph-augmented transformers, Gene Ontology-based neural networks, transcriptomics models enriched with NCBI text embeddings... Historically though, the "Bitter Lesson of AI"[^bitter] has warned us about trying to improve our machine learning models by hand-crafting complex custom rules to constrain the model, and to instead let them learn a useful representation by scaling the compute and data with a properly defined objective. *But Biology is special...* Or is it?

Option (a), we do not have enough data to scale yet, so we have to rely on priors as a crutch, for now at least. Option (b), we strongly value models that validate our priors about Biology, so we try to engrave these priors in our pipelines. Option (c), these priors are necessary because the data alone does not contain the necessary information. Option (d), these priors don't really help but leak in the benchmarks (use GO prior, then benchmark for GO term classification). If I don't know which option is the right one, this to me reveals a very interesting tension in the domain, and a striking difference with SSL approaches elsewhere. In NLP or in vision, models have been trained to fit the structure of the data, with pretraining objectives unapologetically simple to state: data is the ground truth and we build a model of it. In computational Biology, the data does not seem trusted the same way: there seem to be a special need to incorporate various hand-crafted priors to drive the modeling, and I have yet to see any proof that this helps at all.

And this connects to the first sections: what are we building these models for? Do we want models that are optimized to fit the what we know about Biology, or should we always consider data as the ground truth, even if models of it become abstract and diverge from our current understanding? And if some priors can indeed survive scale, then where should they be encoded (in the architecture? loss? data curation and mixture?) All these questions show the cultural friction between machine learning and biomedical applications, and I feel like we’re far from having a final answer. I also expect that a lot of what we bake into our priors could be deduced from multimodality, so this to me should come naturally with time and better approaches. Personally, I'd argue for an Occam's razor approach during pretraining (don't bake any additional priors into a general learner unless you can be fairly certain that it helps), and explore options to augment the model with priors in post-training.
