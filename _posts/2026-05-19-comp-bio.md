As I’ll be moving away from computational biology for at least some time, I wanted to reflect a bit about my journey in the field and where I hope it moves next. For context, I joined in 2018 during an internship with Andrei Zinovyev at Institut Curie, and did my PhD under his supervision between 2020 and 2023. At that time, we were excited about things like adapting auto-encoder architectures to RNA-seq data for dropout denoising[^dca], integrating single-cell expression across patients[^integration], or predicting RNA velocity from unspliced transcripts[^velocity]. Most of the methods back then were based on classical modeling, sometimes borrowing deep learning pieces here and there, and were generally grounded in hypotheses specifically tailored to the data and the problem.

Fast forward to 2026, many canonical ML fields have had their "GPT moment” and were able to successfully tame the transformer architecture to unlock genuine transformative applications by scaling data and compute. Despite the efforts of top labs and brilliant researchers, computational biology is not yet one of them. The literature has been flooded with attempts over the last few years to generalize the LLM pretraining recipe  to transcriptomics data (=throw every data available into a large transformer with a SSL objective, and let it cook), and we have yet to observe striking benefits of this approach over canonical methods such as gene selection + PCA.

![The streetlight effect]({{ '/assets/images/streetlight-effect.jpg' | relative_url }})
*LLM light is bright, but the key may not be there.*

So before they fade away, I have gathered a handful of opinions, ideas and questions I still have in mind which may (or may not) be relevant to build the next generation of models. Maybe some of them will resonate!

## We urgently need to standardize model evaluation

Now that the dust has settled a little bit, it might be worth taking some time to really think about the real-life use cases of these models. How will they be leveraged by practitioners and businesses to bring value? Embed samples to train classifiers? Extract patient trajectories? Discover new biomarkers? Identify new cell types? And is it even possible to get all of these into one model? Ask 5 model makers, get 7 different answers; everyone in the field is super excited about tring a new dataset or a different model architecture, yet no standard evaluation exists to measure progress.

I believe that currently, the most valuable contribution you can bring to the field is a standardized open benchmark that specifies one of the problems we are trying to crack with these models; vision had ImageNet, NLP had GLUE, structural biology had CASP... What do we have? There have been notable attempts here and there like scIB[^scib], or the virtual cell challenge, but they stayed at the fundamental level and nothing has really stuck yet. I understand that working on a benchmark is not super sexy in comparison to developing a new model, but this would go a long way in terms of what the field can achieve in the next few years. I do not pretend this is easy; defining benchmark tasks requires to properly specify what we want to optimize these models for, which as aforementioned is not clear at all right now! But this reflexion is an unskippable step if we want the field to evolve from "proof of concepts" to genuinely useful models.

At Scienta, we built an I&I benchmark[^scienta-bench] to pin down concrete needs and define tasks with real added value; it should be released publicly later this year. I hope to see the field grow a benchmark culture in the coming years. I am convinced that we won't get very useful models if we stick to tasks like cell type prediction on CELLxGENE[^cellxgene], transcriptomics perturbation featuring the same aberrant, 67-chromosome K562 cell line, or cherry-picked claims over unreleased internal evaluations. Without systematic benchmarking, we do not have a clear direction, and we cannot measure any improvement or compare approaches; as we say in France, “*autant pisser dans un violon*” (pointless effort).

## Model pretraining objectives deserve more attention

Many of us have rushed headlong into the LLM pretraining recipe, rounding corners and adding a few convenient hypotheses here and there to fit the transcriptomics data in (no blame, it was 100% the first reasonable thing to try). But now we see that the results aren't great, and if you think a bit: BERT[^bert] learns text embeddings from non-vectorized data, and is used as a text encoder. GPT[^gpt] learns next token prediction and is the core of conversational agents. DINO[^dino] learns well-behaved embedding spaces for image data, and is used as an image encoder.

Transcriptomics foundation models? They learn coexpression patterns between genes, and we hope that it will incentivize the model to "learn the latent Biology". But, what's the rationale here? How does modeling gene-gene coexpression motivate the model to structure a well-behaved, higher-level latent space, encoding things such as tissue state, clinical covariates, cell processes, etc? This comes back to the tasks we're wanting to tackle with these models. If we want to discover new gene-gene interaction patterns, maybe such coexpression pretraining objective can make sense -- though the added value is not super clear compared to fitting a coexpression matrix. For higher-level tasks such as learning a rich sample/patient embedding space that would behave better than the gene expression space, the transfer is way less obvious; why would the model come up with anything richer than encoding the gene-gene coexpression patterns? We may be trying to disguise giant coexpression matrices into GPT-like general learners.

This raises the natural question: then, how do we define SSL pretraining objectives that force the model to construct higher-level representations that are relevant to downstream applications? Can we even chase a "Foundation model of Biology", with a task holistic enough that it captures many intertwined information layers, such as NTP in NLP? Or will the field need to build different models for different use cases, because you can't simultaneously promote good pathway and patient representations? I don’t know the answer, of course, but as you'll see in the next sections, I'd put a coin on moving away from the gene-as-a-token LLM inspiration, and instead explore vision-derived approaches.

## Gene-as-a-token may not be a free lunch

Several approaches recently, such as CellTok[^celltok] and Arc’s STACK[^stack], have been exploring representations of cells that would be more suited for transformers than the “gene as a token” paradigm, replacing that with “cell tokens” or “gene modules”. On the objective side, *Hidden in Plain Sight*[^hidden] suggests that instead of masking individual genes, we should instead mask entire gene groups based on covariance patterns in order to build higher-level relationships between gene expression modules, in the same way that you want to mask a contiguous patch in an image rather than random pixels here and there.

Defining a better-behaved, coarser-grained representations of the transcriptome represents to me the way forward for transcriptomics foundation models, similar to what BPE did in NLP, or VQ-VAE in vision. Add to that the prohibitive cost of performing full self-attention across the whole 20,000+ gene transcriptome, and you get why these recent approaches in transcriptomics make a strong case. And ideally, you'd want an unsupervised, automatic algorithm similar to BPE in NLP.

But how? Transcriptomics is both continuous and unstructured, unlike text and image, so NLP and vision approaches do not cleanly transfer. You cannot easily rely on local structure like in BPE ("merge most frequent bigram") or in VQ-VAE ("map each patch embedding to the closest codebook word"). Without spoiling the whole thing, all I can say is that the team at Scienta has been working hard on this topic lately, and there are some things in the pipeline for later this year (so I'll leave it as a teaser)!

## Why should we use LLMs as a blueprint in the first place?

Probably because of the hype generated by the huge successes in NLP, most of the transcriptomics foundation models are based on the LLM paradigm, yet transcriptomics is arguably closer to vision than it is to natural language. Many things don't translate cleanly from NLP to transcriptomics: one is a structured sequence of discrete tokens, the other is a noisy bag of redundant, unstructured transcripts. In vision, you get some vectorized data as an input with some level of noise, and you want models to learn useful, stable embeddings of these data that you can leverage with downstream models for higher-level tasks. That to me looks like exactly the specifications of a transcriptomics foundation model!

| Feature                  | NLP             | Vision               | Transcriptomics    |
| ------------------------ | --------------- | -------------------- | ------------------ |
| Native input form        | Discrete tokens | Continuous           | Continuous         |
| Vectorized out of the box| No              | Yes                  | Yes                |
| Measurement noise        | None            | Moderate             | High               |
| Needs normalization?     | No              | Yes                  | Yes                |
| Upstream tokenization    | Required (BPE)  | Optional (VQ-VAE)    | Open question      |

So, if SSL approaches like contrastive learning, Barlow Twins[^barlow], DINO, etc., are known to be harder to train than their LLM counterparts due to training instability and hyperparameter sensitivity, it looks like they proved useful tackling data and problems very analogous to the ones we’re facing with transcriptomics. There have been some attempts here and there, but nowhere near the traction pulled by LLM-like approaches, though I'm expecting to see this statement revisited in the coming years.

The main difficulty I think for these approaches is to clearly define what's an *augmentation*; in vision you can crop, reshade, darken, blur, or rotate your image, while in transcriptomics the valid transformations are less obvious, as the data is less interpretable and more noisy. I have seen BYOL and Barlow-Twins adapted to single-cell data with encouraging results[^ssl-theis], using distributional resampling and dropout as augmentations. This, to me, is a promising lead to follow.

## Data is not the primary issue

Unlike others in the field, I’m not too gloomy about what we are able to do with the data available, if we can ever come up with better pretraining objectives. Thanks to massive open-source initiatives, model makers can access bulk and microarray samples in the millions (ARCHS4[^archs4]), and single cells in the hundreds of millions (CELLxGENE[^cellxgene], scBaseCount[^scbasecount]). Sure, more data will come gradually with time and corpora will grow, sure transcriptomics is noisy and redundant, sure perturbation data would be a great addition to teach models causality. Still, I think we already have a plentiful of data to build a first generation of useful foundation models, that have been exposed to many biological conditions, tissues, cell types, species, diseases, technologies, etc.

Most notably, I have noticed that many model makers report that they do not conduct a full pass on their pretraining corpus, and while some teams have reported scaling behavior with dataset size, model size, or compute here and there, the validity of these claims is not settled in the community to this day. If more data or more compute were the bottleneck, I'd expect teams to routinely report clear compute-optimal scaling laws, and a connection between pretraining validation loss and downstream tasks. As it's not the case for now, this reinforces my stance that we are far from making the best out of what's available, mainly due to the limitations in the modeling part (data representation and pretraining objective). Either the data is extremely redundant (partly true for single-cell data, much less for bulk/microarray), or our pretraining tasks are too simple and do not incentivize models to learn the deep data structure. In both cases, the single biggest lever today is not “we need more data”.

So, I would go as far as saying that if we aren’t able to train any satisfying model at current data scales, then the picture looks pretty grim for the future of foundation models for Biology. My impression is that we absolutely need to crack the modeling and transfer learning part of the problem first, before we can scale with more compute and more data.

## Sutton-pilled or prior-pilled, choose your poison

Adding inductive bias to the sauce is becoming increasingly trendy, as a bandaid to circumvent our modeling and data issues: knowledge graph-augmented transformers, GO-based neural networks, biology-specialized LLMs... Historically though, the "Bitter Lesson of AI"[^bitter] has warned us about trying to improve our machine learning models by hand-crafting their priors, and to instead let them learn the optimal representation by scaling the compute and data with a properly defined learning task. *But Biology is special...* Or is it?

Option (a), we do not have enough data to scale yet, so we have to rely on priors as a crutch, for now at least. Option (b), we strongly value models that validate our priors about Biology, so we try to engrave these priors in our pipelines. Option (c), these priors are necessary because the data alone does not contain the information. If I don't know which option is the right one, this to me reveals a very interesting tension in the domain, and a striking difference with SSL approaches elsewhere. In NLP or in vision, models have been trained to fit the structure of the data, with pretraining objectives unapologetically simple to state: data is the ground truth and we build a model of it. In computational Biology, the data does not seem trusted the same way: there seem to be a special need to incorporate various hand-crafted priors to drive the modeling, and I have yet to see any proof that this helps at all.

And this connects to the first sections: what are we building these models for? Do we want models that are optimized to fit the what we know about Biology, or should we always consider data as the ground truth, even if models of it become abstract and diverge from our current understanding? And if some priors can indeed survive scale, then where should they be encoded (in the architecture? loss? data curation and mixture?) All these questions show the cultural friction between machine learning and biomedical applications, and I feel like we’re far from having a final answer. Personally, I'd argue for an Occam's razor approach during pretraining (don't bake any additional priors into a general learner unless you can be fairly certain that it helps), and explore options to augment the model with priors in post-training.

## And what about multimodality?

Finishing with the hardest one. Biological data is multimodal -- everybody agrees on that, and that considering only one modality at a time leaves a lot of signal on the table. But does it mean that there is no hope of training capable unimodal encoders, because the information is too partial, and we should immediately focus our efforts on multimodality? A part of the field defends this thesis, and indeed it's clear that observing a sole transcriptome is insufficient to drive precise patient-level predictions. Or is having good unimodal encoders first a necessary stepping stone before moving on to multimodal models? Currently, that's what most model makers are doing, and there is a strong case for it: sure, we won't get a great patient picture from one transcriptome, but we can probably still build a very useful model of gene-gene interactions, pathways, and covariates involving genomics, proteomics or epigenetics using solely transcriptomics data.

If I can see a case for both, CLIP[^clip] showed us that aligning spaces from two good encoders is a workable recipe. Also, generating paired multimodal data in Biology is much more complex and expensive than scraping co-occurring image-text pairs à la CLIP. To me, this is a pragmatic reason why it's worth pushing a bit more to make unimodal work first: it's a good way to derisk the whole paradigm and nail the pretraining recipes, without committing to a major cost upfront.
